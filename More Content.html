BMI (BODY MASS INDEX)

What is BMI?

Body mass index (BMI) is a tool that healthcare providers use to estimate how much body fat you have by measuring your height and weight. It can be useful in assessing risk factors for certain health conditions.

For most people, BMI is correlated with body fat (the higher the number, the more fat you may have), but in some cases it is not very accurate. BMI alone does not determine health.

What is BMI used for?

Health care providers use BMI and other tools and tests to assess health status and safety risks in people. Too much body fat can lead to heart disease, stroke, and type 2 diabetes. Too little body fat can be linked to malnutrition. This index helps determine the right amount of body fat relative to vitamins and minerals. It also determines how much energy your body uses, helps maintain body temperature, and protects your organs from obesity.

BMI calculation

To calculate your body mass index, follow these steps in order:

  1. Find your weight in kilograms.
  2. Find your height in meters and square it.
  3. And then divide the weight by the height obtained.

Identifying Weight Types and Potential Health Risks with BMI

In general, the BMI ranges listed in the table below classify different weight types:

BMI STATUS HEALTH RISK
Below 18.5 Underweight Minimal
18.5 - 24.9 Normal Minimal
25 - 29.9 Overweight Increased
30 - 34.9 Obese High
35 - 39.9 Severely Obese Very High
Above 40 Morbidly Obese Extremely High
BMI is not the only factor that providers use to classify weight types, other factors include:
  • Waist circumference measurement
  • Measuring skinfold thickness using a folded caliper in specific areas of the body, such as the back of the arms and under the shoulder
  • DEXA scan and ADP air displacement plethysmography
